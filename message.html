Energy Resources

  • Fossil fuels supply ~80% of global energy — coal, oil, and natural gas. Burning them releases CO₂, the main driver of climate change.
  • Nuclear power — low carbon but produces radioactive waste; risk of accidents (Chernobyl, Fukushima).
  • Renewables (solar, wind, hydro) — growing rapidly but variable output; require grid storage solutions like batteries.
  • Biomass — burning organic material; carbon-neutral if managed sustainably, but land-intensive.
  • Energy mix varies by country — Norway is nearly 100% hydroelectric; Saudi Arabia relies on oil.

Water as a Resource

  • Only 3% of Earth's water is fresh, and most of that is locked in glaciers and ice caps. Less than 1% is accessible for human use.
  • Water stress occurs when demand exceeds supply — affects 40% of the world's population.
  • Causes of scarcity: population growth; irrigation (agriculture uses 70% of global freshwater); climate change; pollution; unequal distribution.
  • Virtual water — the hidden water used to produce goods (1 kg of beef requires ~15,000 litres of water).
  • Water conflicts — the Nile basin (Ethiopia's Grand Renaissance Dam), the Colorado River (USA/Mexico), the Aral Sea disaster.

Environmental Issues

🌡️

Climate Change

Burning fossil fuels releases CO₂ and methane, enhancing the greenhouse effect. Average temperatures have risen ~1.1°C above pre-industrial levels. Effects: extreme weather, sea level rise, species extinction, crop failure, displacement.

🌳

Deforestation

12–15 million hectares of forest are lost annually — driven by agriculture (especially beef and soy in the Amazon), logging, and urbanisation. Consequences: CO₂ release, biodiversity loss, soil erosion, disrupted water cycles.

🐟

Ocean Pollution

8 million tonnes of plastic enter oceans each year. Oil spills, agricultural runoff (causing dead zones), and microplastics accumulate in food chains. Ocean acidification from CO₂ dissolving in seawater destroys coral reefs.

🏜️

Desertification

The expansion of desert-like conditions into previously productive land — driven by overgrazing, poor irrigation, deforestation, and climate change. The Sahel region of Africa is a major affected area.

🌿

Biodiversity Loss

The 6th mass extinction is underway — driven by habitat destruction, pollution, climate change, invasive species, and overexploitation. Scientists estimate species are going extinct 100–1,000× faster than the natural background rate.

♻️

Sustainable Development

The UN defines sustainable development as meeting "the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs." Requires balancing economic growth, social equity, and environmental protection.

Causes of Uneven Development

Historical Causes

  • Colonialism — European powers extracted resources from colonies for centuries, leaving exploited economies; arbitrary borders divided ethnic groups and created instability
  • The Scramble for Africa (1880s) — Africa's resources fuelled European industrialisation while leaving African nations underdeveloped
  • Slave trade — removed millions of people from Africa, disrupting societies and economies for generations
  • Unequal trade relationships — post-independence, many countries remained economically dependent on former colonisers, selling raw materials cheaply and buying expensive manufactured goods

Current Causes

  • Geographic factors — landlocked countries, tropical disease burden, lack of natural resources, vulnerability to natural disasters
  • Political instability — civil wars, corruption, poor governance; conflict destroys infrastructure and drives away investment
  • Debt — many LICs (Low Income Countries) owe more in debt repayments than they receive in aid
  • Trade barriers — HICs impose tariffs that prevent LIC manufactured goods from competing
  • Brain drain — skilled workers emigrate to HICs, depriving developing nations of doctors, engineers, and teachers

Aid and Development Strategies

💊

Short-term Aid

Emergency/humanitarian aid for disasters — food, medicine, shelter, water. Provided by governments, NGOs (e.g. Oxfam, MSF), and UN agencies. Fast but doesn't address root causes.

🏗️

Long-term Development Aid

Projects to build infrastructure (roads, schools, hospitals), improve governance, support agriculture, and develop local industries. Aims at sustainable, lasting improvement.

🤝

Bilateral vs Multilateral Aid

Bilateral = country to country (often with conditions). Multilateral = through international organisations (World Bank, IMF, UN). Multilateral seen as more impartial.

📈

Trade vs Aid

"Trade not aid" — fairer trade rules, removing subsidies in HICs, and giving LICs access to markets is argued to be more effective than aid. E.g. Fairtrade certification.

🏦

Loans & Microfinance

World Bank and IMF loans can fund development but often come with conditions (structural adjustment). Microfinance gives small loans to entrepreneurs in LICs — empowers women especially.

🎯

UN Sustainable Development Goals

17 SDGs adopted in 2015 — a global blueprint for development by 2030. Includes ending poverty, zero hunger, quality education, gender equality, clean energy, and climate action.

Case Study — Ethiopia: One of the world's poorest nations (GNI per capita ~$1,000) but has shown rapid economic growth (10% pa for a decade). Challenges include drought, land-locked geography, and political conflict in Tigray. International aid, Chinese infrastructure investment, and growing manufacturing are driving development. Remains an HDI of 0.498 — low human development.

Economic Development Stages

Rostow's Stages of Economic Growth (1960)

  • Stage 1 — Traditional society: Subsistence agriculture, limited technology. e.g. parts of sub-Saharan Africa
  • Stage 2 — Pre-conditions for take-off: Commercial agriculture, some manufacturing, improved infrastructure. e.g. Ethiopia now
  • Stage 3 — Take-off: Rapid industrialisation; manufacturing expands; urbanisation accelerates. e.g. China 1980s–2000s
  • Stage 4 — Drive to maturity: Diversified economy; modern technology throughout; reduced poverty. e.g. Mexico, Brazil
  • Stage 5 — Mass consumption: High income; service-dominated; consumerism. e.g. USA, Germany, UK, Japan

Globalisation & Trade

What is Globalisation?

Globalisation is the process by which the world's economies, cultures, and populations have become increasingly interconnected and interdependent through trade, investment, technology, and migration.

Key drivers: containerisation (cheap shipping), the internet, deregulation of trade, growth of TNCs (Transnational Corporations), and falling communication costs.

TNCs — Transnational Corporations

TNCs are companies that operate in multiple countries. They include Apple, Nike, McDonald's, and Shell. TNCs provide jobs and investment in LICs but often move profits back to HICs, use cheap labour, and have significant environmental impacts.

Advantages for host countries: jobs, technology transfer, improved infrastructure, tax revenue.
Disadvantages: low wages, poor conditions, profit repatriation, environmental damage.

🏭

Deindustrialisation

The decline of manufacturing in HICs as factories move to countries with cheaper labour (e.g. China, Bangladesh). Led to unemployment and social deprivation in former industrial cities (e.g. Detroit, Sheffield).

🌏

Newly Emerging Economies (NEEs)

Countries that have rapidly industrialised and are transitioning from primary to secondary/tertiary — BRICS (Brazil, Russia, India, China, South Africa). China now has the world's 2nd largest economy.

🤖

The Knowledge Economy

In post-industrial HICs, growth comes from quaternary industries — IT, finance, research, creative industries. Skills and education matter more than physical labour. Drives demand for highly qualified workers.

⚖️

Fair Trade

Guarantees minimum prices for producers in LICs, ensuring fair wages and safe conditions. Coffee, chocolate, and bananas are key fair trade products. Criticised by some for market distortions.

🏦

Free Trade vs Protectionism

Free trade removes tariffs and barriers — WTO promotes this. Protectionism uses tariffs/quotas to protect domestic industries. HICs often subsidise their own agriculture while demanding LICs open markets.

🌐

Informal Economy

Unregulated, untaxed economic activity. In LICs, 60–80% of employment can be informal — street vending, domestic work, small-scale services. Workers have no rights or social protection.

The Demographic Transition Model (DTM)

5 Stages of the DTM

Stage 1 — High Stationary

High birth rate AND high death rate → slow population growth. Pre-industrial societies. Very high infant mortality; no reliable contraception; children needed for labour. e.g. remote tribal communities today.

Stage 2 — Early Expanding

Death rate falls (improved sanitation, medicine, food supply) but birth rate remains high → rapid population growth. e.g. Sub-Saharan Africa, parts of South Asia today; UK in early 19th century.

Stage 3 — Late Expanding

Birth rate begins to fall (urbanisation, education for women, contraception, lower infant mortality) while death rate continues to fall → population still grows but more slowly. e.g. Brazil, India in transition.

Stage 4 — Low Stationary

Both birth rate and death rate are low → low population growth. e.g. UK, USA, France today. Ageing population becomes the main challenge.

Stage 5 — Decline

Birth rate falls below death rate → natural population decrease. e.g. Japan, Germany, Italy. Immigration may offset declining population.

Population Policies

Anti-natalist Policies (reducing birth rate)

  • China's One Child Policy (1980–2015): Limited most urban families to one child. Fines for violations; state pressure. Reduced birth rate but created gender imbalance (preference for sons), ageing population, and ethical abuses.
  • India's Family Planning Programme: Education, contraception provision, and financial incentives for sterilisation. Less coercive than China but some forced sterilisations reported.
  • Iran: Reversed from pro-natalist to anti-natalist and back again based on government priorities.

Pro-natalist Policies (increasing birth rate)

  • France: One of the most successful pro-natalist programmes — generous maternity/paternity leave, childcare subsidies, tax benefits for families with 3+ children. Birth rate has risen to 1.84.
  • Sweden: Generous parental leave (up to 480 days shared between parents), universal childcare. Birth rate stabilised.
  • Singapore: Cash grants ("baby bonuses"), tax rebates, childcare subsidies. Earlier anti-natalist policies caused population decline that required reversal.
  • Hungary: Mortgage subsidies, lifetime income tax exemptions for mothers of 4+ children.

Migration

Push Factors

Reasons why people leave a place: poverty, unemployment, war and conflict, natural disasters, persecution, climate change, famine, lack of services (healthcare, education).

Pull Factors

Reasons why people are attracted to a destination: better jobs, higher wages, political stability, safety, better education and healthcare, family reunification, more freedoms.

Economic Migration

Moving primarily to improve economic opportunities. e.g. Eastern Europeans to UK post-2004 EU expansion; Mexicans to USA; Indian IT workers to Silicon Valley.

Refugees & Asylum Seekers

Refugees flee persecution, war, or disaster — legally protected under 1951 UN Refugee Convention. By 2024, over 100 million forcibly displaced people worldwide — the highest ever recorded.

Impacts on Origin Country

Negative: brain drain (loses skilled workers); reduced workforce. Positive: remittances (money sent home — $800B+ annually globally, more than total aid).

Impacts on Host Country

Positive: fills labour shortages; cultural diversity; pays taxes; entrepreneurship. Negative: pressure on services; housing; potential social tensions if integration is poor.

Urbanisation: Over half the world's population now lives in cities — a figure expected to reach 70% by 2050. Urbanisation is fastest in Africa and Asia. Megacities (populations over 10 million) include Tokyo (37M), Delhi (33M), Shanghai (29M), and Lagos (15M). Rapid urbanisation creates challenges: housing shortages, slums (informal settlements), traffic congestion, pollution, and overstretched infrastructure.

Causes of Global Inequality

  • Colonial legacy — exploited economies, drawn borders, resource extraction for centuries
  • Unfair trade rules — HICs subsidise agriculture; LICs cannot compete; tariffs on LIC manufactured goods
  • Debt burden — some LICs spend more on debt repayment than healthcare or education
  • Conflict — destroys infrastructure, disrupts development, creates refugees
  • Geography — landlocked nations, disease burden, climate vulnerability
  • Corruption and poor governance — aid and resources lost to corruption rather than reaching people

Consequences of Global Inequality

  • Preventable disease and malnutrition kill millions annually
  • 811 million people go to bed hungry every night
  • 150 million children stunted from chronic malnutrition
  • Inequality drives migration and refugee crises
  • Political instability and conflict more likely in highly unequal societies
  • Environmental damage — poorest countries most vulnerable to climate change they contributed least to

Global Conflicts

💣

Causes of Conflict

Resource competition (water, oil, land), ethnic/religious differences, political power struggles, inequality, colonial border legacies, nationalism, and competition for strategic territory.

🌍

Types of Conflict

Civil war (intrastate), interstate war, terrorism, genocide, proxy wars (superpowers funding opposing sides). Many modern conflicts are hybrid — combining conventional warfare and terrorism.

🕊️

Consequences of Conflict

Death and injury; displacement (100M+ forcibly displaced globally); economic destruction; healthcare and education collapse; psychological trauma; regional destabilisation; refugee crises.

🏛️

Role of International Organisations

UN Security Council authorises peacekeeping missions (90,000+ UN peacekeepers deployed). NATO provides collective defence. ICC prosecutes war crimes. WHO coordinates health responses in conflict zones.

⚡

Resource Conflicts

Water — Nile basin disputes; South China Sea disputes over fishing/oil; Congo wars partly driven by minerals (coltan in phones). "Resource curse" — mineral-rich countries often have more conflict, not less.

🛡️

Peacebuilding

Post-conflict: disarmament, demobilisation, reconstruction; transitional justice (truth and reconciliation commissions); institution building; economic recovery; education for peace. Rwanda's recovery since 1994 genocide is a remarkable case study.

Food Security & Hunger

The Food Security Challenge

Food security exists when all people, at all times, have physical, social, and economic access to sufficient, safe, and nutritious food. Currently, 811 million people are undernourished, while 2 billion are overweight or obese — the "double burden of malnutrition."

Climate Change as a Global Issue

Global Impact of Climate Change

  • Sea level rise — threatens low-lying nations (Maldives, Bangladesh, Pacific island states); 1B people live within 10m of sea level
  • Extreme weather — stronger hurricanes, prolonged droughts, more severe floods; 2023 was the hottest year ever recorded
  • Climate refugees — by 2050, up to 1 billion people may be displaced by climate change
  • Arctic amplification — Arctic warming at 3× the global average; permafrost thaw releases methane
  • Ecosystem collapse — coral reef bleaching; Amazon dieback; loss of boreal forest; species extinction

International Climate Response

  • Paris Agreement (2015) — 196 countries agreed to limit warming to 1.5–2°C above pre-industrial levels through nationally determined contributions (NDCs)
  • COP meetings — annual UN climate conferences. COP28 (2023) first agreement to transition away from fossil fuels
  • Net zero targets — UK targets net zero by 2050; EU by 2050; China by 2060 (though still building coal plants)
  • Climate finance — $100B/year promised by rich nations to help poor nations adapt. Most targets not met.
  • Loss and damage — breakthrough at COP27/28: fund established for countries suffering climate damages they didn't cause

Health as a Global Issue

🦠

Infectious Disease

Diseases like malaria (600,000 deaths/year), tuberculosis (1.6M/year), and HIV/AIDS (650,000/year) disproportionately affect LICs. COVID-19 killed 7M+ globally and exposed deep health inequalities between rich and poor nations.

💉

WHO & Global Health

The World Health Organisation coordinates international health response — disease surveillance, vaccine programmes, health standards. Funded by member states. Its COVID response was criticised as slow; praised for polio eradication progress.

🏥

Health Inequalities

People in LICs are far more likely to die from preventable diseases. 400M people lack basic healthcare. A child born in Sierra Leone is 80× more likely to die before age 5 than one born in Norway. Access to medicines is also deeply unequal.

The UN & International Cooperation: The United Nations (193 member states) is the primary forum for international cooperation. Key bodies: UN General Assembly (all nations equal vote), UN Security Council (15 members, 5 permanent with veto power: USA, UK, France, Russia, China), UN Human Rights Council, UNESCO, UNICEF, UNHCR (refugees), WFP (food). The UN's effectiveness is limited by the Security Council veto and reliance on member state funding and consent.
